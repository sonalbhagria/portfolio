The brief

A platform that had outgrown itself

The business problem

The platform had been built on a legacy system that was never designed for scale. It was desktop-only, not CMS-driven, and had accumulated years of design debt across five distinct products — each with its own information architecture, interaction patterns, and visual language.

For 930+ organisations across 60+ countries, this was the primary tool for intelligence, risk assessment, and economic forecasting. The stakes of getting migration wrong were high. Enterprise clients had invested years learning the existing system. Disrupting that familiarity risked churn at exactly the moment we needed to build confidence in the new platform.

The brief was deceptively complex: modernise everything — responsiveness, CMS infrastructure, design language, navigation — while ensuring that users who knew the old system could find their way in the new one without relearning from scratch. Before a single wireframe was drawn, I ran UX audits across the platform and identified $200K+ in revenue at risk from broken and confusing journeys — giving the business a commercial reason to invest in onboarding redesign, not just visual modernisation.

I led UX strategy across all five products, designed the component system that would underpin the new platform, ran the research that shaped each product's migration approach, and managed alignment with product, engineering, and senior stakeholders throughout a three-year phased delivery.

The challenge

Modernise everything. Disorient no one.

The central design tension

Enterprise users are not casual users. The analysts, economists, and risk managers using this platform had built their daily workflows around it. They knew exactly where to go, what to click, and how to get what they needed — even if the system that enabled this was technically outdated.

Familiarity is a form of trust. Breaking it — even in service of improvement — can feel like a betrayal to users who have invested time in learning a system. The research was clear: users were not asking for change. They were asking for stability, with better performance underneath.

This shaped every design decision. The new platform needed to feel like an evolution, not a replacement. Navigation patterns had to remain recognisable. Content hierarchy had to be preserved. The visual language could modernise, but the mental model had to stay intact.

Where we did introduce new capabilities — responsive layouts, improved filtering, CMS-driven content updates — we designed them to feel like natural extensions of what users already knew, not additions that required learning.

"The hardest design problem isn't building something new. It's changing something familiar — without the users noticing the change, only feeling the improvement."

Scope

Five products. One phased migration.

What was migrated — and why sequencing mattered

Each product had a different user base, a different content model, and a different set of familiarity risks. Migrating them in sequence — rather than all at once — let us learn from each rollout, refine the approach, and carry those lessons into the next product. It also meant that at any given moment, the majority of users were on a stable platform while migration was underway.

The flagship product. Highest traffic, most established user behaviour. Migrated first to set the template for all others.

Approach

Research, design, migrate, learn.

The three phases that structured every product migration

01
Research & familiarity mapping
Understanding what users knew — before changing anything
Before each product

What I investigated

Usage patterns

  • Which navigation paths were most used — and therefore highest risk to change
  • Which features users had learned to work around rather than use properly
  • Where users expressed frustration vs where they expressed confidence
  • Which analytics signals correlated with renewal risk — cross-referencing usage data with account health to identify where UX failure was becoming a commercial problem

Research methods

Mixed methods

  • Analytics review to identify high-traffic paths and drop-off points
  • User interviews with representative enterprise accounts
  • Usability testing of migration prototypes before any engineering work began
  • UX audits to surface revenue risk from broken or confusing journeys

Output

A migration risk map

  • Ranked list of UX elements by familiarity risk — what could change vs what had to stay
  • Design principles specific to each product's user base
  • Onboarding strategy tailored to the delta between old and new experience
→

Why this phase mattered

Every migration decision started with data, not assumption. Knowing which elements were load-bearing for user confidence meant we could be bold where we had room to be, and conservative where we didn't. This phase is what made 89% adoption achievable.

02
Design system & component migration
The connective tissue that made five products feel like one platform
Ran in parallel

The problem it solved

Five products, five design languages

  • Each legacy product had evolved independently — no shared components, no shared patterns
  • Migration was the moment to fix this — but only if the design system was built before products migrated, not after
  • Without a system, each product migration would create new inconsistency rather than resolve old inconsistency

What I built

EIU's first design system

  • Full component library covering navigation, data display, filters, content cards, and interactive states
  • UX governance documentation so components could be extended without breaking consistency
  • Accessibility standards baked in from the start — not added retrospectively
  • Figma-to-engineering handoff protocols that reduced iteration cycles

The result

One system, five products

  • All five migrated products share a single component library
  • New feature work across any product builds on existing components — not from scratch
  • Design-engineering handoff friction reduced significantly
  • The system has been extended, not replaced, for all work since 2023
03
Onboarding redesign & adoption strategy
The difference between launching a new platform and users actually adopting it
At each product launch

How I identified the risk

$200K+ revenue at risk — found through analytics

  • I cross-referenced platform analytics with account health data — looking for enterprise accounts where usage was dropping during the migration window
  • UX audits of the journeys those accounts were dropping out of revealed broken flows and confusing transitions that weren't visible in aggregate data
  • The gap between launching a new platform and users adopting it is where churn happens
  • For enterprise clients with annual contracts, early confusion in migration could trigger non-renewal

What I designed

Guided transition UX

  • Contextual onboarding that activated only at the moments users needed it — not as a blanket tour
  • In-product wayfinding that bridged old navigation patterns to new ones
  • Re-engagement flows for inactive enterprise accounts identified through analytics
  • A/B tested onboarding variants to identify highest-converting approach

The outcome

Adoption that held

  • 89% adoption within 90 days — beating the 60-80% enterprise benchmark
  • 67-70% monthly return rate sustained post-migration
  • Churn signals in at-risk accounts reduced after re-engagement flows launched
  • 8-15% MoM feature engagement growth as users discovered capabilities in the new platform
→

The lesson

The migration experience didn't end at launch. How users were brought across — what they saw first, what was explained and what wasn't, what happened when they got lost — determined whether adoption was a spike or a sustained behaviour change. Designing for the transition was as important as designing the destination.

Reflection

What I'd do differently

Honest retrospective

The phased approach was the right call, but the gaps between phases were too long in the middle of the programme. Products 02 and 03 had a longer interval between their migrations than was ideal — users who spanned both products experienced inconsistency for longer than necessary. If I were doing this again, I'd push harder for tighter sequencing even at the cost of some scope reduction per phase.

I'd also have invested in a shared research repository from day one. Each product migration generated significant user research, but it lived in separate files and wasn't systematically accessible across squads. A shared insights library would have made each subsequent migration faster and better-informed.
